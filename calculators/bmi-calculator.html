How to Calculate BMI

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple numerical value derived from your weight and height. It is widely used as a screening tool to categorize individuals into weight groups — underweight, normal weight, overweight, and obese.

BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ Height (m)²

Where:

  • Weight is measured in kilograms (kg). If using pounds, multiply by 0.453592 to convert.
  • Height is measured in meters (m). If using centimeters, divide by 100. If using feet/inches, convert to total inches and multiply by 0.0254.

Example: A person weighing 70 kg with a height of 175 cm (1.75 m) has a BMI of 70 ÷ (1.75 × 1.75) = 70 ÷ 3.0625 = 22.86, which falls in the "Normal Weight" category.

Frequently Asked Questions

BMI (Body Mass Index) is a ratio of your weight to your height squared. It matters because research shows a strong correlation between BMI and body fat percentage in most populations. While it's not a direct measure of body fat, it serves as an easy-to-calculate screening tool for potential health risks associated with being underweight, overweight, or obese — including heart disease, diabetes, and hypertension.

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), a healthy BMI range for adults is between 18.5 and 24.9. A BMI below 18.5 indicates underweight, 25.0 to 29.9 indicates overweight, and 30.0 or above indicates obesity. However, healthy ranges can vary slightly by ethnicity — for example, some Asian health authorities recommend a lower threshold (23.0) for overweight classification.

BMI has several limitations: it does not distinguish between muscle mass and fat mass, so muscular athletes may be classified as overweight despite having low body fat. It also doesn't account for age, gender, bone density, or fat distribution. Waist circumference, waist-to-hip ratio, and body fat percentage tests (such as DEXA scans) provide more comprehensive assessments of body composition and health risk.

BMI is calculated the same way for children and teens, but the interpretation is different. For individuals aged 2–20, BMI is plotted on age- and sex-specific growth charts to obtain a percentile ranking. A child at or above the 85th percentile is considered overweight, while the 95th percentile and above indicates obesity. This age-adjusted approach accounts for normal changes in body composition during growth and development.
